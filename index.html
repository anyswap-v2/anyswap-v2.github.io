What is Anyswap?

Anyswap began as a cross-chain decentralized exchange and bridge project, then relaunched as Multichain with a broader cross-chain router focus. The official Multichain FAQ says the old Anyswap domain would receive no more updates after the rebrand. Today, the name is best understood as legacy infrastructure and history, not a fresh bridge to use casually.

How Anyswap worked

Anyswap and Multichain used bridge and router patterns to move token representations across chains. The user-facing idea was simple, but the trust model depended on contracts, liquidity, and MPC-controlled signing.

  1. Lock or route assetsA user sent assets into a bridge address or router pool on the source chain.
  2. MPC nodes observed the eventThe protocol relied on a secure multi-party computation node network to coordinate signing and cross-chain execution.
  3. Mint, release, or pool-matchDepending on the asset type, the destination side could mint a wrapped token, release pool liquidity, or leave a pool-share token to redeem later.
  4. Users tracked completionFinal delivery depended on chain confirmations, destination liquidity, token support, and the health of the bridge service.

Current status and risk

The important modern context is not only how Anyswap was designed, but what later happened to Multichain operations.

Rebrand context

The official docs state that Anyswap relaunched as Multichain and that the original Anyswap site would have no more updates. Use the FAQ as historical source material, not as proof that a route is currently usable.

Service halt

After unauthorized withdrawals and operational issues, Chainalysis reported that Multichain ceased operations and described the event as a possible hack or insider rug pull. Read the Chainalysis incident analysis before treating any legacy bridge contract as low-risk.

Bridge exposure

Cross-chain bridges can concentrate assets and depend on signing, verifier, liquidity, or custody assumptions. Ethereum.org's bridge overview is a useful neutral primer on why these designs need extra review.

Tokens and fees

Anyswap content often leads to old ANY, MULTI, or wrapped-token references. Treat those references as historical unless a current, reputable source confirms support.

ANY to MULTI

The Multichain docs describe ANY to MULTI as a one-way conversion at a fixed token ratio through a web portal. That legacy instruction appears in the official FAQ, but it should not be read as a current safety endorsement.

Fee components

There is no universal Anyswap fee to quote here. A bridge or router transaction can involve source-chain gas, destination-chain gas, token approval gas, protocol routing charges, and liquidity effects shown by the interface before signing.

Audit trail

Historical audits and reports are available in the Anyswap-Audit GitHub repository. Audits are useful inputs, but they do not remove operational, key-management, liquidity, or governance risk.

Anyswap FAQ

Is Anyswap still active?

Treat Anyswap as a legacy brand. The official Multichain FAQ says Anyswap relaunched as Multichain and that the original Anyswap domain would have no more updates. Later, Chainalysis reported that Multichain ceased operations after unauthorized withdrawals and operational failures.

Did Anyswap become Multichain?

Yes. The official FAQ describes Anyswap as founded as a cross-chain decentralized exchange and relaunched as Multichain to focus on cross-chain infrastructure. That rebrand is the main reason searches for Anyswap often lead to Multichain docs, token pages, and incident reports.

How did the Anyswap bridge model work?

The bridge model locked assets on one chain and minted or released corresponding assets on another chain, with SMPC nodes coordinating the cross-chain action. The official bridge docs describe the lock, mint, burn, and release pattern used for wrapped assets.

What happened to Multichain?

Multichain suffered unauthorized withdrawals and then ceased operations, according to Chainalysis' incident analysis. Chainalysis framed the withdrawals as a possible hack or rug pull and noted problems around CEO access, MPC keys, and operational control. That history is central to any Anyswap risk review.

Are ANY and MULTI the same token?

They are connected historically, not casually interchangeable everywhere. The Multichain FAQ described an ANY to MULTI conversion at a fixed ratio through a one-way process. Because the broader protocol later halted, do not assume an old token-swap instruction is currently usable or low-risk.

What should I check before touching old Anyswap assets?

Start with contract addresses, chain, liquidity, issuer status, bridge backing, wallet approvals, and whether any redemption path still exists. Ethereum.org's bridge overview explains why bridge assets carry extra trust assumptions. For Anyswap or Multichain assets, also read the Chainalysis incident analysis first.
